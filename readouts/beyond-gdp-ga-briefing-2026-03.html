Executive Summary

Two months before the Expert Group's final report, PGA Baerbock convened an informal plenary briefing to update member states on progress. Co-Chairs Basu and Lustig presented an interim framework: a dashboard of ~30 indicators (~50% drawn from the SDGs), organised around well-being, equity/inclusion, and sustainability, anchored by foundational principles of peace, human rights, and respect for the planet.

This was a briefing, not a negotiation — and the tone reflects that. Every delegation that took the floor accepted GDP's insufficiency; none defended GDP as adequate. The fault lines that dominate the May consultation are visible here only in embryonic form: the LMG MICs already push finance-link language, but no delegation draws an explicit blocking line. Critically, the positions that hardened into the May skeptical bloc — Russia's GDP-primacy stance, China's "objective indicators only" demand, and the CANS anti-finance line — are absent or silent at this meeting. Saudi Arabia and China spoke but their statements were inaudible (podium audio fault); their positions are not on the record here.

The time-series story. Read against the 7 May consultation, this briefing is the "before" picture: broad, soft consensus around a process everyone welcomes. The opposition had not yet mobilised. The value of this readout is precisely that contrast.
Areas of Consensus

This briefing was consensus-heavy. Five threads of agreement run across nearly every intervention:

  • GDP insufficiency — universally accepted. Every delegation that took the floor, including those that later turned cautious, explicitly agreed GDP alone is inadequate.
  • SDG complementarity, not duplication — near-universal. The 50% SDG overlap in the proposed dashboard was welcomed by Poland, Kenya, Japan and others.
  • Country ownership & member-state drive — all delegations stressed indicators must be country-owned, nationally applicable, and GA-driven, not externally imposed.
  • Statistical capacity investment — a cross-bloc demand (Philippines/LMG, Angola, Ethiopia, Kenya, Lao PDR, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Australia, Zimbabwe): ambition must be matched by investment in national statistical systems.
  • A process, not a fixed product — Co-Chair Lustig framed this explicitly: "This has to be seen as a process. Our recommendations should not be seen as something that is unmovable." Poland, Kenya and Japan echoed the iterative approach.
Supportive Blocs
  • LMG MICs (Philippines) delivered the strongest finance-link statement of the meeting — invoking the Rabat and Makati Declarations and the Sevilla Commitment, demanding metrics that "inform access to development finance, including concessional finance" and interoperability with IFI policies.
  • Latin American MICs (Colombia, Panama, Mexico) align with the LMG line; Colombia frames Beyond GDP as "essential," Panama poses operational "who decides" questions, Mexico brings 20+ years of multidimensional-poverty experience.
  • African Group (South Africa, Kenya, Ethiopia, Angola, Zimbabwe) strongly supportive — South Africa via the Ubuntu principle and its G20 presidency agenda; Zimbabwe with the meeting's clearest finance-protection language ("support, rather than constrain, our access to development finance").
  • EU / Western supporters (Spain, Austria, Poland) back the work and spotlight the Beyond GDP Global Alliance (Spain/OECD/UNCTAD, launched at FfD4); Spain becomes a May co-facilitator. Their support is framed around measurement and sustainability — not concessional finance.
  • Morocco is supportive and procedurally engaged — the first delegation to explicitly request a roadmap from the co-chairs.
Cautious or Conditional Support
  • Pakistan gives conditional support with guardrails: practical feasibility, national-context sensitivity, methodological soundness, alignment with SDGs, and "beyond-GDP must not replace GDP altogether." Notably, its finance-link position is silent here — absent given its sharper May positioning.
  • Japan is constructively cautious — welcomes the interim indicators and an "open, transparent and inclusive process," but flags data-availability and statistical-capacity gaps as a comparability challenge.
  • Australia holds the meeting's most nuanced finance-link position: supports multidimensional metrics but insists any financing criteria be "context-specific," declining to endorse blanket linking — the earliest trace of the CANS reservation that hardens by May. Strongly backs the MVI for Pacific small states.
  • Bangladesh, Lao PDR support the direction but stress flexibility, country-led design, no new ranking systems, and that the effort "must not become a costly endeavor" without technology transfer.
  • Switzerland supportive in principle (delivered partly in French); GDP is "a key indicator… but not sufficient to guide policy in a complex world."
Skeptical or Blocking Positions
No skeptical or blocking bloc was present at this briefing. This is the single most important finding of the time-series comparison. Not one delegation on the record drew a red line, rejected the framework's premise, or defended GDP primacy. The positions that crystallise into the May skeptical bloc were either silent or absent here:
  • Russian Federation — did not take the floor. By May it becomes the most contrarian voice (GDP must remain principal; "subjective" metrics unacceptable).
  • China — spoke but was inaudible (podium audio fault). Its on-record May position (objective/quantifiable indicators only; no single prescriptive model) is not attributed here. See the inaudible-card note in the reference section.
  • CANS anti-finance line — present only as Australia's "context-specific criteria" caveat. The explicit "must not inform international financing" position does not yet exist on the record.
  • Pakistan's foundational-principles challenge — at this meeting Pakistan is cautious, not skeptical; its sharper questioning of the peace/human-rights principles appears only by May.

Coding note: the absence of skepticism is a finding, not a gap. Forcing March voices into a skeptical bucket would fabricate hardening that had not yet occurred.

Policy and Implementation Implications

At this pre-launch stage the fault lines are latent. Three to watch as they harden toward May:

1. The Finance Split — embryonic. The LMG MICs (Philippines) explicitly tie the framework to concessional-finance reform, citing Rabat, Makati and Sevilla; Colombia, Morocco, Angola, Zimbabwe and Bangladesh echo variants. No delegation yet opposes it — the eventual blocking position (CANS) is silent. The defining May tension exists here only as a one-sided demand.
2. Indicator subjectivity — latent. Co-Chair Basu acknowledges subjective well-being is "very difficult to measure, but very important." No delegation rejects subjective indicators; the Russia/China resistance is silent. Only Pakistan's "methodologically sound" caveat hints at the coming debate.
3. Governance venue — not yet contested. All delegations assume the GA is the right venue; none proposes routing technical governance to the Statistical Commission. This axis is effectively dormant — its activation by May is itself a signal of hardening.
Earliest signal to monitor: Morocco's direct roadmap request — "how does the panel envisage structuring its output so that member states can meaningfully engage?" — is the first on-record version of the roadmap demand that becomes cross-bloc by May (EU, African Group, Brazil, South Africa, Sudan, China).

Issues and Challenges Raised
  • Statistical capacity & financing for statistics: the most-repeated concern — Philippines/LMG, Angola, Ethiopia, Kenya, Lao PDR, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Australia and Zimbabwe all stress that ambition without funded data systems is rhetorical.
  • Risk of duplication: Philippines/LMG, Mexico, Pakistan, Bangladesh and Lao PDR warn against overlap with the SDGs, MVI, HDI and the multidimensional poverty index.
  • Roadmap absent: Morocco asks directly how the output will be structured for meaningful engagement — the first roadmap demand.
  • Operational definitions: Panama asks who decides which economic activity genuinely contributes to well-being versus compensating for structural problems; Mexico asks whether the group has identified political challenges to implementation.
  • Data availability & comparability: Japan and Australia flag uneven data across countries as a threat to comparability and to small-state inclusion.
  • Transcript gaps: Saudi Arabia and China were inaudible (podium audio fault); their positions are not recorded here and must not be inferred.

  • DSG MohammedOutlined four next steps: (1) report launch end of April in New York; (2) member-state engagement via RC offices and regional commissions; (3) intergovernmental process post-launch; (4) early on-the-ground action with UN country teams. Offered an updated fact sheet to fill gaps before release.
  • Expert Group (Basu & Lustig)Final report confirmed on track for April/May. Dashboard ~30 indicators, ~50% from the SDG framework; exploring 2–3 headline composite indicators alongside GDP. Lustig stressed outcome indicators only; enablers tracked separately.
  • Statistical CommissionHigh-Level Forum on Official Statistics (57th StatComm side event) held 2 March — the day before this meeting — engaging national statisticians on the framework.
Talking Points
"The framework should consider interoperability with the policies of the international financial institutions… a more concrete link between the new framework and the reform of the current international debt architecture, which deprives MICs of concessional finance."
— Philippines, for the LMG MICs: the meeting's strongest finance-link statement, anchored in the Rabat and Makati Declarations and the Sevilla Commitment
"This has to be seen as a process. Our recommendations should not be seen as something that is unmovable."
— Co-Chair Nora Lustig: framing the Expert Group's output as iterative, not final — a tone that defuses early skepticism
"Any new framework should support, rather than constrain, our access to development finance and our ability to pursue nationally determined priorities."
— Zimbabwe: the clearest articulation of the Global South finance-protection concern that the framework not become a new conditionality
"Any international eligibility or allocation criteria should be context-specific, with indicators that are targeted to the development issue at hand."
— Australia: the earliest trace of the CANS reservation against blanket finance-linkage that hardens by May
Reference: Stances by Nation/Group

Philippines (LMG MICs)

Supportive · LMG MIC · Finance-pro

Strongest finance-link statement of the meeting. Invokes Rabat/Makati Declarations, Pact Action 53, Sevilla Commitment; metrics must inform access to development finance; wants IFI interoperability and a link to debt-architecture reform.

"…a more concrete link between the new framework and the reform of the current international debt architecture, which deprives MICs of concessional finance."

Colombia

Supportive · LMG MIC · Finance-pro

Strongly supportive; "an essential advance to update how we understand and orient development." Stresses natural capital and structural vulnerabilities invisible to GDP. Partly in Spanish.

"La adopción del Comité de Sevilla reafirmó la necesidad de avanzar hacia métricas más allá del PIB que faciliten el acceso al financiamiento…"

Morocco

Supportive · G77 · Finance-pro

Supportive and procedurally engaged; first to request a roadmap. Wants balanced three-pillar coverage and policy — not just statistical — recommendations that inform financing.

"…broader policy recommendations… able to inform decision-making and how budgets are allocated… and how access to development finance is managed."

Angola

Supportive · African Group · Finance-pro

Three principles: national ownership central; coherence not fragmentation; framework must recognise multi-dimensional vulnerabilities to inform financing and debt sustainability.

"…one that recognizes multi-dimensional vulnerabilities and better informed decisions on financing, debt sustainability and development support."

Austria

Supportive · EU/Western · Finance-silent

Fully supportive; highlights the Beyond GDP Global Alliance (Spain/OECD/UNCTAD) and ODA graduation reform for SIDS. Support framed around measurement, not concessional finance.

"Austria particularly appreciates the outreach and inclusivity that the group has pursued…"

Pakistan

Cautious · G77 · Finance-silent

Conditional support with guardrails: practical feasibility, national context, methodological soundness, SDG alignment; "beyond-GDP must not replace GDP altogether." Finance-link silent.

"Indicators must be methodologically sound and aligned with existing frameworks, particularly the sustainable development goals."

Saudi Arabia

Inaudible · Arab/G77 · No position recorded

Statement inaudible — podium audio fault. Content unrecoverable beyond fragments. No stance is on the record; coding is a placeholder only and must not be inferred.

[Transcript inaudible — audio fault at podium. No position recorded.]

South Africa

Supportive · African Group · Finance-silent

Strongly supportive via the Ubuntu principle and its G20 presidency agenda (inclusive growth, debt relief, climate, critical minerals). GDP "ignores" communal well-being. Finance-link silent here.

"…the inextricable link between individual care and communal well-being, something that… a focus on GDP, ignores."

China

Inaudible · G77/P5 · No position recorded

Statement inaudible — podium audio fault. Its on-record May position (objective indicators only; no single model) is NOT attributed here. No March stance recorded; must not be inferred.

[Transcript inaudible — audio fault at podium. No position recorded.]

Switzerland

Cautious · Western Europe · Finance-silent

Supportive in principle, four structured observations (partly in French). GDP a key indicator but insufficient for complex policymaking; complement not replace.

"Le PIB… ne suffit pas à orienter les politiques dans un monde complexe. Nous… compléter et non… remplacer le PIB…"

Spain

Supportive · EU / Alliance co-lead · Finance-silent

Highly supportive. Announces the Beyond GDP Global Alliance (Spain/UNCTAD/OECD) as a Sevilla deliverable; stresses aligning finance and policy with equity, resilience, sustainability. Becomes a May co-facilitator.

"Spain took the initiative, along with UNCTAD, OECD and SEGIB… to lead the creation of the Global Beyond GDP [Alliance], launched in the framework of the Action of Sevilla."

Kenya

Supportive · African Group · Finance-pro

Four asks: inclusive/transparent process; SDG complementarity; statistical-capacity strengthening; broader metrics to inform financing and cooperation. Cites Bottom-Up Economic Transformation Agenda.

"…broader development metrics should help inform international financing, development cooperation, and policy decisions…"

Japan

Cautious · OECD / Friends of Chair · Finance-silent

Constructively cautious. Welcomes the 7 interim example indicators and an open, inclusive process; flags data-availability and statistical-capacity differences as a comparability challenge.

"…we should be mindful of the differences in data availability and the statistical capacity across countries, as these can influence results and their comparability."

Mexico

Cautious · LMG MIC · Finance-silent

Constructive, empirical. 20+ years of multidimensional-poverty measurement; review existing tools before new metrics; asks the group whether it has identified political challenges. Quality over quantity.

"Before proposing new metrics, it is very important to review with rigor the information and tools available…"

Ethiopia

Supportive · African Group / LDC · Finance-silent

Strongly supportive; notes integration of broader metrics into policy has stayed limited despite decades of effort — this is "a considerable shift." Highlights AI/emerging tech for data.

"Its systemic integration mainly depends on robust and innovative data sources leveraged in emerging technologies, including AI."

Lao PDR

Cautious · LDC / ASEAN · Finance-silent

Cautiously supportive; global recommendations should remain flexible, country-led, adaptable. Calls for technical cooperation, methodological guidance, capacity support.

"Global recommendations should remain flexible, country-led, and adaptable to the national context…"

Poland

Supportive · EU / Eastern Europe · Finance-silent

Supportive and constructive; frames Poland's own transformation as GDP-necessary-but-not-sufficient. Welcomes the 50% SDG overlap; asks how HDI can be strengthened to feed Beyond GDP.

"…approximately 50% of indicators drawn from the SDG indicator framework is a good beginning of the conversation about… complementarity…"

Australia

Cautious · CANS / Pacific · Finance-conditional

Most nuanced finance-link position: supports multidimensional metrics but financing criteria must be "context-specific," declining blanket linkage — earliest trace of the CANS reservation. Strongly backs the MVI for Pacific small states.

"Any international eligibility or allocation criteria should be context-specific, with indicators that are targeted to the development issue at hand…"

Panama

Supportive · LMG MIC · Finance-pro

Supportive; advancing beyond GDP is "not a technical exercise" but a step to expand the data behind policy. Poses operational questions: how to define cases where GDP is inappropriate; who decides what activity contributes to well-being.

"Avanzar a métricas que complementen o superen el PIB no es un ejercicio técnico…"

Zimbabwe

Supportive · African Group · Finance-pro

Supportive with clear Global South framing: country ownership; coherence with 2030 Agenda; practical dashboard. Clearest finance-protection language of the meeting; calls for statistical-capacity investment.

"Any new framework should support, rather than constrain, our access to development finance…"

Bangladesh

Cautious · LDC / G77 · Finance-pro

Final speaker; cautiously supportive. Additional measures "critical" but must not replace GDP; warns against ranking systems and a costly effort without cooperation and technology transfer.

"…Otherwise, capacity gaps could widen further, and the risk of misrepresentation would rather increase."

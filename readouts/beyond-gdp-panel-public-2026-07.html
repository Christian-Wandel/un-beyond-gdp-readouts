Read this first — what this panel was
A 50-minute moderated side event at the High-level Political Forum on Sustainable Development (HLPF) 2026 in New York, titled "Beyond GDP: Moving from Analysis to Impact." One moderator, three seated panelists (Mexico, UK, UNDP), two invited expert statements (civil society, philanthropy), and four audience interventions. Speakers appear as practitioners and experts stating their own views on the record — not formal national positions. This readout maps the implementation fault lines among the people in the room; speakers silent on a fault line are simply not shown on it.
Executive summary

Two months after the report-launch consultation, the Beyond GDP conversation has shifted from whether to move past GDP to how to make it operational. Consensus is broad — GDP is insufficient, well-being metrics belong in real policy machinery, national ownership is paramount — but the panel exposed real disagreement about execution.

Most consequential takeaway — procedural: Spain (co-facilitating with Guyana) has opened a call for input to an "elements paper," deadline 10 August 2026, shaping the next phase toward a possible General Assembly resolution. Details below.

Three live fault lines about execution:

  • Pace — move fast to capture momentum, or take the time consensus needs? →
  • Metrics or finance — is Beyond GDP a measurement agenda, or fundamentally about access to finance? →
  • Index or dashboard — converge on a headline number, or stay a 31-indicator dashboard? →

A fourth thread — whether the exercise is technocratic or human-centred — ran through the panel as a recurring talking point rather than a hard dividing line.

Process news — the actionable item

Elements paper: call for input is open

Deadline: 10 August 2026

Announced on the record by Ignacio García, Minister-Counsellor of the Spanish Mission, co-facilitator of the intergovernmental process (Spain with Guyana). Open to member states and stakeholders/civil society, via the UN Beyond GDP process page. Four guiding blocks:

  • Political vision of the Beyond GDP process
  • Technical indicator questions, including possible headline indicators beyond the 31-indicator dashboard
  • National implementation — the tension between a universal framework and country ownership
  • Governance and links to other processes (Sevilla commitment, 2030 Agenda, and the Multidimensional Vulnerability Index / MVI)

The elements paper "will open the following phase of this process, which should eventually lead to some kind of resolution by the General Assembly."

Where they agree — four points of consensus
  • GDP is insufficient but not discarded. Every speaker treats Beyond GDP as complementing GDP, not replacing it. Nobody's talking about replacing GDP. GDP will always be there. This is about complementing GDP. — Ignacio García (Spain)
  • Well-being metrics belong in real policy machinery. The UK integrates them into Treasury "Green Book" cost-benefit guidance; Mexico links 21 of the report's 31 indicators to its national statistics institute (INEGI). We'd see it as providing the quality assessment and qualitative understanding behind what drives sustainable and inclusive growth. — Grace Wood (UK)
  • National ownership and relatability. The agenda must be country-owned and action-oriented; citizens actually relate to it. Many people relate to it… which, many UN topics, you know, it's not there. — Livia Bizikova (IISD)
  • This is an opening, not a finished product. A generational, years-long agenda — not a report to file. This is not a final product. This is an opening of the door. — George Gray Malena (UNDP)

Fault lines — where the room divides
Each fault line below is one live disagreement about implementation, shown as the speakers who took a position on it and their verbatim words.

1 · Pace

Should the agenda move fast to capture existing momentum, or take the time an intergovernmental consensus needs?

The sharpest live tension of the panel. Bizikova warns that countries' existing adjacent capacities — SDG offices, natural-capital accounting, green-growth units — will dissipate without quick consolidation. The diplomat and the UN economist both counsel patience.

Livia Bizikova (IISD)Act now

"If we don't act quickly on the Beyond GDP agenda… they will disappear, and the countries, they may feel that, oh, now it's coming Beyond GDP and I'm starting over again."

George Gray Malena (UNDP)No clock

"It will take its own sweet time because there is no clock on this. This has been happening for years. It will continue to happen."

Rodolfo Reta Hadad (Mexico)No clock

"Consultations on issues that are so inherent to human perspectives cannot be done in six months a year."

2 · Metrics or finance — what the agenda is really about

Is Beyond GDP a measurement-and-policy agenda, or fundamentally a question of access to finance?

The same structural fault line the May consultation exposed across blocs — now voiced from inside the practitioner community. Mexico names finance as the substance of the entire discussion; the UNDP's chief economic adviser publicly retires his own earlier metrics-first optimism.

Grace Wood (UK)Metrics & policy

"We'd see it as providing the quality assessment and qualitative understanding behind what drives sustainable and inclusive growth."

George Gray Malena (UNDP)Shifted toward finance

"Maybe five years ago, I myself was a little more naive in thinking that if we just had the right metrics, we would move the capital markets… first we need to figure out what access to capital markets is all about."

Rodolfo Reta Hadad (Mexico)Finance first

"The discussion has been solely about how a group of countries can get access to concessional finance, development finance, technical capacity, and other types of resources."

3 · Index or dashboard

Should the framework stay a 31-indicator dashboard, or converge on a set of headline summary measures?

The "where's the number?" question from the report's press reception is now a formal item: headline indicators are one of the elements paper's four guiding blocks. Only two speakers took an on-record view — but it is now formally posed to member states, the clearest rising question in the silo.

George Gray Malena (UNDP)Dashboard, open to more

"What we have is a dashboard. But it may well emerge over the next few years — and I would not discount that we come up with summary measures in the future."

Ignacio García (Spain, co-facilitator)Headline indicators

"Complementing GDP with 31 indicators is fine, but perhaps a set of headline indicators as well."
